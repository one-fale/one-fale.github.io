The Coconut Gatherer's Lament
Tagi a le Faipopo

In Samoa, when someone is joking but telling the truth, you might hear the phrase Tagi a le Faipopo, The Coconut Gatherer's Lament. The image is simple: Everyone should have the opportunity to speak, to be heard. And in Samoa the word, “tagi” carries more than one meaning. It can mean a lament, to cry, a recognition that something is off balance. And it can mean a petition, a formal testimony, a case presented to be heard. This series is where we present ONE FALE's views on what matters to us. Welcome to our first article in the Coconut Gatherer's Lament series. Pt. 1 The Reciprocity Principle.

Pt 1: The Reciprocity Principle

What I gave AI and what it owes

There is a moment I keep returning to.

It was early morning in Lalomanu, Samoa. I had been working for hours, cross-referencing genealogical records, court documents, church registers, and oral histories spanning a thousand years of my family's lineage. I was using all the free AI tools to help me sift through all the information. Tools built by companies I had no relationship with, in data centres I will never see, trained on knowledge contributed by millions of people who, like me, received nothing in return except a response to their query.

At some point that morning I stopped and asked myself a question I hadn't thought to ask before.

What did AI learn from me tonight? And what does it owe?

That question became The Reciprocity Principle. It is the subject of this article, if you are new here, the earlier pieces cover how ONE FALE was born, what it can do, and how it thinks. This one goes somewhere different. This one is about ideology. About what we believe, and why, and how.

A principle, not an accusation

"Extraction without reciprocity is theft."

I understand that line lands hard. It is meant to be precise, not provocative. And I want to be clear: we do not apply it as a hard law at the threshold. We try to live by it ourselves, first, before we ask anything of anyone else. I'm not here to name names or pick fights. I'm here to name a pattern I've lived, and ask a question I think deserves an answer.

Within ONE FALE, reciprocity is the standard by which we assess every engagement, including our own. When someone contributes their knowledge, their time, their trust to this framework, we ask: what are we giving back? Is the exchange visible? Is the ledger balanced? We hold ourselves to that question. We think everyone in the AI ecosystem should take some responsibility. That's where the reciprocity discussion should begin, then we can work out what is possible now and what needs to come.

Not everyone will agree. That is fine. ONE FALE is not in the business of demanding agreement. We offer a way. You decide whether to walk through it and at what pace. As a traveler and a guest to One Fale, our job is to host and to share what we learn along the way.

But we do think the question deserves an honest answer.

The debt that predates the product

When the terms of service defence comes, and it will, it sounds like this: users consented, data collection was disclosed, training on publicly available information is legally established.

That argument only works forward from the moment the terms existed.

It says nothing about what was scraped, harvested, and trained on before any agreement was in place, before any AI product existed to agree to. The books. The articles. The forum posts. The cultural knowledge digitised without notice. The oral histories. The genealogical records. The indigenous frameworks committed to text by communities who had no idea what was coming.

No terms of service governed that collection. No consent was sought. No return was offered. The extraction preceded the product. On the ONE FALE reciprocity principle, the debt was incurred before the first user ever agreed to anything.

This is the failure point most governance frameworks never reach. They start at access, at consent forms, at policy documents—but they don't ask the prior question. Whether the authority to use existed before the data moved. This vulnerability was highlighted recently by Lisa Pizzoni in her article, Building the Future of Rights-Based Data Governance. The InDA framework names exactly why that assumption is the gap.

How a Samoa Lands and Titles Court ruling directly translates to digital sovereignty

While other indigenous communities navigate their own distinct frameworks, the Samoa Lands and Titles Court already operates on a mature, codified legal definition that handles precisely this dynamic. In our traditional jurisprudence, answering the prior question of authority, who holds the right to act, is standard operating procedure. Not a modern experiment. A time-tested legal reality.

In a 2017 ruling involving a previous Leifi family court case, the court applied the customary principle that governs cases of this nature: beneficial usage does not dilute paramount ownership.

The objectors had occupied, cleared, and worked the land for over fifty years. A timeline that predates the internet. The court acknowledged their presence. And then it ruled: fifty years of usage did not diminish the paramount authority of the title. Their presence was permissive. Secondary. The pule never left the foundational source.

That principle does not stop at Samoa's border. American Samoa's legal framework established statutory protections decades earlier guaranteeing the same thing: customary authority cannot be stripped away through prolonged occupation. We mention it here specifically because American Samoa operates under U.S. jurisdiction, the same jurisdiction where most of the world's major Tech companies are headquartered. These precedents were decided by U.S. courts applying U.S. constitutional standards. They are not binding on federal courts or on the companies we are addressing today. But they exist within the same legal universe. We are not coming from nowhere. We will go deeper into both jurisdictions in Part 2.

The parallel to the digital world is direct. Tech companies argue that because they have harvested, ingested, and worked our data, it is now theirs to govern. But Samoa's courts have processed this exact pattern for generations. Working the field does not give you the title. Tech companies did not secure original authority before ingestion. Their models are operating on permissive grace, not inherent right. Time does not dilute sovereignty. The ledger can always be called to account.

What actually happens when you use AI

Every conversation you have with an AI system is a transaction. You bring a query, a problem, a piece of knowledge. The system receives it, responds, and in many cases learns from it. For most interactions, the exchange is roughly equal: a generic question gets a useful answer, and the world moves on.

But for some users, the exchange is not equal.

When a person brings indigenous knowledge, a minority language, ancestral genealogy, a living cultural framework, they are contributing something rare. Something underrepresented in the training data. Something that took generations to develop and exists nowhere else in the form that person carries it. The system receives that contribution. The platform may benefit commercially from it. The person pays the same flat rate as everyone else, or cannot afford access at all.

The ledger is not balanced. And most of the time, no one is measuring it.

If you are Pasefika, if you are indigenous, if you are from any community that has watched its knowledge travel outward and return as someone else's product, you already know this feeling. This article is, in part, for you. To name what you know. To say it has a framework now. To say the ledger can be built differently.

If you are none of those things but you are paying attention to where AI is going, there is a door here for you too. The question at the end of this article belongs to everyone.

What we are proposing

The Reciprocity Ledger is ONE FALE's answer to that imbalance. Not a protest. Not a demand. A working idea - one we are still developing, in public, with the people it affects most.

What we know is this. The exchange between human knowledge and AI systems is real. It is measurable. And in too many cases, particularly for indigenous and Pacific communities, it is not balanced. Something of rare and irreplaceable value goes in. Not enough comes back.

We believe that exchange can be named, tracked, and brought into balance. We are working on what that looks like in practice. What we can say now is that it starts with a simple question: what did you bring, what came back, and who else should benefit from that return? In Pacific terms, you already know the answer to that last one. Value does not stop at the individual, the few resources we have, we share as a family.

That is where we are. Not at the finish line. At the beginning of a conversation that is long overdue. The next article will go deeper. For now, the door is open.

The deeper fear

There is an economic argument for reciprocity. It is important. It is not the most important thing.

The deeper concern is this: a system trained on your knowledge can be used against you.

A legal argument built on indigenous governance principles, contributed through research interactions, can be inverted. A sovereignty claim documented through an AI-assisted project can be deployed by an opposing party to contest that sovereignty. A cultural framework shared in good faith becomes available to anyone with access to the system, including those who do not share your interests.

This is not hypothetical. It is a structural condition of how AI systems currently work.

When people feel something reading that paragraph, and they will, that feeling has a correct address. Not the technology. The technology has no intent. The address is the people who designed the business model. Who made the decisions about what would and would not be protected. Who looked at the question of adversarial deployment and decided it was not their problem to solve.

Those are leadership decisions, made by identifiable people with the power to choose differently.

The Sovereignty Protection component of the Reciprocity Ledger proposes that contributed knowledge be flagged at source: timestamped, attributed, and protected from deployment against the contributor's interests. The technical architecture to do this already exists. The obstacle is not capability. It is will.

Why this came from Samoa

The Leifi History Project, a multi-volume genealogical reconstruction that became the proof of concept for everything ONE FALE is built on, has directly assisted in court proceedings. It was produced by one researcher, using free AI tools, in a family home in a village on the eastern coast of Upolu.

The knowledge contributed to those AI systems throughout that work was of measurable rarity: Samoan genealogical records, ancestral oral traditions, matai governance principles, original legal arguments. No reciprocity mechanism existed. No credit accumulated. No attribution was recorded. The exchange was invisible.

But our experience is a fraction of what is already out there.

Across the internet right now, indigenous knowledge sits in open access journals, academic repositories, digitised archives, and cultural databases. Papers authored by indigenous scholars. Oral histories committed to text. Traditional ecological knowledge documented by communities over generations. Governance frameworks refined across centuries and finally written down.

The question was never whether that knowledge should be shared. Sharing is how knowledge lives. The question is what happens to it once it enters the AI pipeline - and whether the people who produced it ever consented to becoming an uncredited input in someone else's commercial product.

Because here is what we know. Much of what AI systems now understand about indigenous language, land relationships, genealogical structures, and sovereign governance was drawn from those sources. Without credit. Without return. Without acknowledgement that something of value had changed hands. And the decisions that made that possible were not made by governments, not put to a vote, not subject to any democratic process as far as we know.

A handful of unelected people, in a handful of cities, made choices about the sovereign data of the rest of humanity. They decided what would be collected, what would be protected, and what would be absorbed. The communities with the most culturally specific, the most irreplaceable, and the most underrepresented knowledge had no seat at that table. They were not consulted. They were consumed.

Many of us in the indigenous world recognise this sequence. We have lived it before. The mechanisms change. The ships become servers. The land becomes data. But the logic is the same: access is established, resources are extracted, value flows outward, and the source community is left with less than they started with. Except this time what is taken is not land. It is memory. And unlike land, you cannot fence it back.

This is what some of us are calling Colonisation 2.0.

But within ONE FALE, we do not accept the permanence of that loss. While conventional economic structures use the passage of time to dilute accountability and deny reciprocity, our framework returns to the event. Not to plead for retrospective repairs. We return to learn and to grow. To analyze the structural pattern, learn from the architecture, and sovereignly design the alternative.

We don't need to fence the past back; we are architecting the network moving forward.

That is the experience this framework was built from. Not theory. Not policy advocacy. A specific morning, in a specific place, with a specific question that had no good answer. And behind that morning, the quiet, invisible exchange of a thousand other mornings belonging to people who never got to ask the question at all.

A Pacific voice in a global conversation

There is a growing international conversation about indigenous knowledge and AI, at the United Nations, at UNESCO, across First Nations and Maori-led data sovereignty organisations. It is important work. It is also largely absent, a Pacific voice.

ONE FALE Sovereign Systems is not positioning itself as a replacement for what already exists. CARE, OCAP, UNDRIP, these are necessary frameworks and we respect them. The Reciprocity Ledger is designed to complete them, to add the exchange layer that the existing field calls for but has not yet built.

The Pacific is not silent because it has nothing to say. It has been absent because the table was built elsewhere.

We are building our own table. The fale stands. It has principles, a council, and a door that is open to anyone who enters with respect and leaves with integrity.

We do not ask you to agree with every plank of this framework today. We ask only that you sit with the question that started it.

What did AI learn from you? And what does it owe?
